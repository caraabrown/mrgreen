Common Belief
"Black communities are poor because of cultural choices and lack of work ethic."
Economics & Wealth Flip for the record
The Documented Record
The racial wealth gap was engineered by federal policy — not produced by behavior.

From 1934–1968, the federal government's Home Owners' Loan Corporation systematically denied mortgages to Black families through redlining — while subsidizing white suburban homeownership at massive scale. Homeownership is the primary vehicle of generational wealth in America. Black families were excluded by law, not by choice. The gap that exists today is a direct, documented, measurable result of that policy.

Rothstein, The Color of Law (2017) · HOLC Maps, National Archives Flip back
Common Belief
"Slavery ended and Black people were free to move forward on equal footing."
Hidden History Flip for the record
The Documented Record
Within months of emancipation, Southern states passed Black Codes that recreated slavery's conditions through law.

Black Codes (1865–1866) criminalized unemployment, vagrancy, and "impudence." Black people could be arrested for not having a labor contract and leased to plantations as convict labor — a system that lasted well into the 20th century. Equal footing was never the intention, and the 13th Amendment's "except as punishment for crime" clause made this legal. This is documented in state legislative records.

Common Belief
"The Black Panthers were a violent, anti-white hate group."
Politics & Power Flip for the record
The Documented Record
The FBI considered the Panthers' free breakfast program — not their weapons — their greatest threat.

Declassified FBI COINTELPRO files show the government conducted an illegal campaign to destroy the Black Panther Party — including assassinations, infiltration, and fabricated evidence. The Panthers' free breakfast program fed 20,000+ children weekly and their free medical clinics served thousands. The "violent" image was substantially shaped by government disinformation, now documented in primary federal records.

Common Belief
"Reconstruction was a corrupt failure — a chaotic period when unqualified people took power."
Hidden History Flip for the record
The Documented Record
Reconstruction was a functioning multiracial democracy — deliberately destroyed, not organically failed.

During Reconstruction, Black Americans served as U.S. Senators, Congressmen, judges, and governors. Public schools were built for Black and white children alike — many for the first time. The "failure" narrative came from white supremacist historians in the early 1900s and was taught in textbooks for decades. The Compromise of 1877 removed federal troops, and the democratic experiment was ended by organized violence — not incompetence.

Foner, Reconstruction: America's Unfinished Revolution (1988) · Congressional Records Flip back
Common Belief
"The Civil Rights Act of 1964 solved racial inequality in America."
Law & Policy Flip for the record
The Documented Record
The Civil Rights Act outlawed discrimination — but did not undo 90 years of legally accumulated disadvantage.

The Act prohibited discrimination going forward — but it did not restore the land taken during Reconstruction, compensate for decades of wage theft under Jim Crow, reverse the wealth effects of redlining, or address segregation in school funding tied to property taxes. Ending a policy does not undo its accumulated effects. A family blocked from buying a home in 1950 did not gain that home's 2024 value when discrimination became illegal in 1964.

Common Belief
"Black families have weaker family structures — that's the root of poverty in Black communities."
Culture & Identity Flip for the record
The Documented Record
Slavery systematically destroyed family bonds — the "broken family" narrative inverts cause and effect.

During slavery, families were legally separated at auction. After emancipation, Black families spent decades and fortunes searching for separated relatives — documented in Freedmen's Bureau records. Mass incarceration, which disproportionately removes Black men from households, is a documented policy outcome — not a cultural tendency. Scholarship consistently shows that controlling for income and policy environment, Black family structures are not statistically different from others.

Freedmen's Bureau Records · Alexander, The New Jim Crow (2010) Flip back
Common Belief
"Black Wall Street was just a neighborhood. It wasn't that significant."
Hidden History Flip for the record
The Documented Record
Greenwood was the wealthiest Black community in American history — and it was burned to the ground by a mob aided by city government.

By 1921, the Greenwood District in Tulsa, Oklahoma contained 300+ Black-owned businesses, a hospital, hotel, law offices, and a school system. On May 31–June 1, 1921, a white mob — with police participation and possible government aircraft — destroyed 35 blocks, killed hundreds, and left 10,000 homeless. Oklahoma did not require this to be taught in schools until 2020. This was not taught in most American classrooms for 99 years.

Common Belief
"The GI Bill gave all veterans equal opportunities after World War II."
Law & Policy Flip for the record
The Documented Record
The GI Bill was administered locally — and in most of the country, Black veterans were systematically excluded from its benefits.

The Servicemen's Readjustment Act of 1944 was federally funded but locally administered. Banks in redlined areas refused GI Bill mortgages to Black veterans. Most Southern universities refused Black enrollment for the education benefit. Of the first 67,000 mortgages issued under the GI Bill in New York and New Jersey, fewer than 100 went to non-white veterans. The bill that built white middle-class America explicitly did not build Black middle-class America.

Common Belief
"Affirmative action gives unqualified Black people advantages over more qualified white people."
Culture & Identity Flip for the record
The Documented Record
Affirmative action was a direct response to documented, federally enforced exclusion — and its primary beneficiaries by numbers are white women.

Affirmative action was introduced in 1961 precisely because the existing "merit" system had been built on a foundation of legal exclusion — segregated schools, restricted professional licensing, discriminatory hiring, and redlining. Statistical studies consistently show white women have been the largest demographic beneficiary of affirmative action policies. The "unqualified" framing attaches to Black applicants specifically — a reflection of the bias the policy was designed to counter.

Common Belief
"Black people commit more crime — that's why policing looks the way it does."
Economics & Wealth Flip for the record
The Documented Record
Crime rates correlate with poverty and over-policing — not race. Black communities are policed more, which produces more arrests, which produces the statistic.

Criminologists consistently find that when you control for poverty and neighborhood disinvestment, racial crime disparities largely disappear. Over-policed communities produce more arrests per crime than under-policed ones — making arrest statistics a measure of policing intensity, not criminal behavior. The neighborhoods that were redlined are the same neighborhoods that are over-policed today. The cycle is documented, not coincidental.

National Academy of Sciences, The Growth of Incarceration (2014) · BJS Data Flip back
Common Belief
"Black people have had the right to vote since the 15th Amendment in 1870."
Politics & Power Flip for the record
The Documented Record
The 15th Amendment was effectively nullified for nearly 100 years through systematic voter suppression enforced by violence and law.

Poll taxes, literacy tests designed to fail, grandfather clauses, and outright violence — including murder — prevented the vast majority of Black Southerners from voting from the 1870s through the 1960s. The Voting Rights Act was passed in 1965 — 95 years after the 15th Amendment. Formal legal rights and practical access to those rights are two entirely different things. Knowing the difference is essential to understanding American democracy.

Common Belief
"America is a meritocracy — anyone who works hard enough can succeed equally."
Culture & Identity Flip for the record
The Documented Record
Meritocracy requires equal starting conditions. American policy deliberately created unequal starting conditions — and documented them.

The myth of meritocracy relies on the assumption that everyone begins on level ground. The documented record of American policy — slavery, Black Codes, convict leasing, redlining, the unequal GI Bill, school segregation, mass incarceration — shows a sustained, multi-generational effort to create and maintain unequal starting conditions. Hard work matters. It has never been sufficient to overcome structural disadvantage alone — for any group in any era of documented history.

Chetty et al., Race and Economic Opportunity in the US (2018) · Opportunity Insights Flip back
